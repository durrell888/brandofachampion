## Step 1: Understand the Timeline

Recruiting doesn't start your senior year. If you're waiting until then, you're already behind.

**Freshman Year (9th Grade)**
- Focus on development. Get stronger, faster, and learn your position.
- Start building your highlight film — yes, this early.
- Attend local camps and combines to start getting evaluated.

**Sophomore Year (10th Grade)**
- This is when college coaches start identifying prospects.
- Create your recruiting profile on platforms like Hudl.
- Start reaching out to college coaches via email (we'll cover how below).
- Attend college camps — this is where coaches see you in person.

**Junior Year (11th Grade)**
- This is the most critical year for recruiting.
- Your junior film is what most coaches evaluate.
- Official and unofficial visits start happening.
- Offers can come as early as the spring of your junior year.
- This is when you need to be actively communicating with coaches weekly.

**Senior Year (12th Grade)**
- Commitments and signing day.
- If you haven't been recruited yet, it's not over — but urgency is high.
- Late bloomers can still find opportunities at D2, D3, NAIA, and JUCO programs.
- National Signing Day in February is the big moment.

## Step 2: Build a Highlight Film That Gets Watched

College coaches receive hundreds of highlight films. Most get watched for less than 30 seconds. Here's how to make yours stand out:

**Do:**
- Lead with your best play. Not your second best. Your BEST.
- Keep it under 5 minutes. Coaches don't have time for 15-minute films.
- Include your jersey number, position, height, weight, and GPA at the beginning.
- Show full-speed plays first, then slow-motion replays.
- Include plays from multiple games, not just one blowout.
- Show plays where you make mistakes and recover — coaches want to see football IQ.

**Don't:**
- Don't add music so loud coaches can't hear the game.
- Don't include plays where you're standing around.
- Don't pad it with plays against weak competition only.
- Don't use flashy intros that waste the first 15 seconds.

## Step 3: Know What College Coaches Actually Look For

We've talked to coaches at every level. Here's what they consistently say matters most:

**On the Field:**
1. Speed and athleticism — can you move?
2. Football IQ — do you understand the game or just play it?
3. Coachability — do you respond to coaching or argue?
4. Competitive toughness — how do you play when things go wrong?
5. Consistency — one highlight play doesn't matter if you disappear the rest of the game.

**Off the Field:**
1. Grades — you can't play if you can't get admitted. NCAA requires a minimum 2.3 GPA for D1.
2. Character — coaches call your high school coach and ask about you as a person.
3. Work ethic — do you show up to offseason workouts?
4. Social media presence — yes, they check. Clean it up.

## Step 4: Start Contacting Coaches (The Right Way)

Don't sit around waiting for coaches to find you. That's not how this works for 95% of recruits.

**Your initial email to a coach should include:**
- Your name, position, high school, graduation year
- Height, weight, GPA, and test scores
- A link to your highlight film (Hudl or YouTube — not a download)
- Your schedule so they know when to come watch you play
- A brief statement about why you're interested in their program specifically

**Example email:**

> Coach [Name],
>
> My name is [Your Name], and I'm a [position] at [High School] in [City, State] (Class of [Year]). I'm [height], [weight], with a [GPA] GPA.
>
> I've been following [University] football and I'm very interested in your program because [specific reason — don't be generic].
>
> Here's my highlight film: [link]
> Here's my upcoming schedule: [list games]
>
> I'd love the opportunity to visit campus and learn more about your program. Thank you for your time.
>
> [Your Name]
> [Phone Number]
> [High School Coach's Name and Number]

**Follow up every 2-3 weeks.** Not every day. Not once and forget it. Consistent, respectful follow-up.

## Step 5: Attend Camps and Combines

College camps are where coaches evaluate you in person. Film is important, but nothing replaces seeing an athlete compete live.

**Types of camps:**
- **College-run camps** — hosted at the university, coached by their staff. These are the most important.
- **Mega camps** — multiple college coaching staffs at one location.
- **Combines** — testing events (40-yard dash, vertical jump, shuttle).
- **Showcases** — competitive settings where scouts evaluate talent.

**Pro tip:** Don't go to 30 camps. Go to 8-10 that match your talent level and interest. Research which coaches will be there.

## Step 6: Get Your Academics Right

This kills more recruiting dreams than anything else. You can be the most talented player in your state, but if you can't qualify academically, none of it matters.

**NCAA Eligibility Requirements (D1):**
- Minimum 2.3 GPA in core courses
- Minimum SAT or ACT score (sliding scale with GPA)
- 16 core courses completed
- Register with the NCAA Eligibility Center (do this sophomore year)

**Our advice:** Treat your GPA like your 40 time. Train it. Improve it. Take it seriously.
