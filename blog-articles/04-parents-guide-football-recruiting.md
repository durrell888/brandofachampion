Your son comes home and says he wants to play college football. Maybe a coach told him he has potential. Maybe he's been dreaming about it since he was eight years old.

Either way, you're now in the recruiting game whether you're ready or not.

And here's the truth that most parents learn too late: the recruiting process is confusing, expensive if you're not careful, and full of people who will take your money without delivering results.

This guide is for you — the parent who wants to help their kid but doesn't want to get played in the process.

## First Question: Is Your Son Actually Recruitable?

This is the hardest conversation, but it needs to happen honestly.

Not every good high school football player is a college football player. And that's okay. But before you invest time, money, and emotional energy into the recruiting process, you need an honest assessment.

**Signs your son is recruitable:**
- He's a starter on a competitive varsity team by junior year
- His high school coach has talked about college potential (without you asking)
- He performs well against quality competition, not just weak opponents
- He has measurables that are at least in the range for his position at the next level
- He has the grades to be eligible (2.3 GPA minimum for D1)

**Signs you might need a reality check:**
- Only family members have suggested he could play college ball
- He's physically behind his peers and not catching up
- His grades make eligibility a serious concern
- He doesn't love football enough to do the work it requires

This isn't about crushing dreams. It's about being realistic so your family can make smart decisions. And remember — D1 isn't the only path. D2, D3, NAIA, and JUCO are all legitimate options where your son can play college football and get a degree.

## The Timeline Parents Need to Know

Most parents start paying attention to recruiting during their son's senior year. That's at least two years too late.

**8th Grade / Freshman Year:**
- Focus is on development, not recruiting
- Start building good academic habits — grades matter more than you think
- Let him play and learn the game
- Your job: make sure he's in a good program with good coaching

**Sophomore Year:**
- Start researching the NCAA Eligibility Center (register at eligibilitycenter.org)
- Help him build a basic highlight film
- Begin identifying schools that are realistic targets
- Attend college camps together — this is a bonding experience AND an evaluation tool
- Your job: be organized, keep a spreadsheet of contacts and deadlines

**Junior Year:**
- This is the most important year. Junior film is what coaches evaluate.
- Help him send emails to coaches (don't send them yourself — coaches want to hear from the athlete)
- Schedule unofficial visits to schools he's interested in
- Make sure his transcript is on track for eligibility
- Your job: be the logistics coordinator, not the agent

**Senior Year:**
- Commitments and signing day
- Official visits (schools pay for these — 5 maximum)
- National Letter of Intent signing
- Your job: help him make a wise decision, not an emotional one

## What Recruiting Actually Costs (And Where You're Getting Scammed)

Let's talk money, because this is where families get taken advantage of.

**Things that are worth paying for:**
- Quality coaching and training ($50-200/month is reasonable)
- A few targeted college camps ($50-150 per camp)
- A professionally edited highlight film ($100-300 one-time)
- A recruiting service with PROVEN results (like Brand of a Champion — $4.99-19.99/month)

**Things that are NOT worth paying for:**
- "National recruiting databases" that charge $500+ and do nothing
- Services that guarantee offers (nobody can guarantee an offer)
- Attending 20+ camps in one summer (diminishing returns after 8-10)
- "Exposure events" that are really just cash grabs with no real coaches present

**Red flags in recruiting services:**
- They guarantee scholarship offers
- They charge thousands of dollars upfront
- They can't name specific athletes they've placed at specific schools
- They pressure you to sign up immediately
- They claim every kid who walks through the door is a D1 prospect

At Brand of a Champion, we can point to Aaron Gregory at Texas A&M, DJ Bordeaux at UCLA, Jordan Carter at Tennessee, and more. If a service can't name names and schools, walk away.

## Your Role as a Parent (What to Do and What NOT to Do)

**DO:**
- Be your son's biggest supporter, not his agent
- Keep a spreadsheet of schools, coaches, contact info, and deadlines
- Drive him to camps, visits, and evaluations
- Make sure his grades stay on track
- Ask questions and stay informed about the process
- Let him own his communication with coaches
- Help him write emails, but don't write them for him
- Be positive on the sidelines

**DO NOT:**
- Call college coaches yourself (unless a coach reaches out to you first)
- Argue with high school coaches about playing time — college coaches WILL hear about it
- Post negative things on social media about coaches, refs, or your son's team
- Compare your son to other recruits publicly
- Pressure your son into choosing a school you want instead of the right fit for him
- Talk badly about other athletes or programs
- Be the parent that coaches warn each other about

That last one is real. Coaches talk. If you're known as a problem parent, it will affect your son's recruitment. Fair or not, that's how it works.

## How to Evaluate a College Program

When offers start coming in, you need to help your son evaluate them beyond just the name on the jersey.

**Questions to ask:**
- What's the graduation rate for football players?
- What's the average time to earn a degree?
- What does the depth chart look like at his position?
- What's the coaching staff's track record of developing players?
- What happens if he gets injured — does the scholarship hold?
- What's the academic support system like?
- What's the culture of the program? (Visit and feel it yourself)
- Can you afford the things the scholarship doesn't cover?

**Remember:** The best school for your son isn't always the biggest name. It's the one where he'll play, develop, earn a degree, and be in an environment that brings out his best.
