College coaches receive hundreds of highlight films every month. Most get watched for 15-30 seconds before they're closed.

Not because the athlete isn't talented. Because the film is bad.

Your highlight film is your resume. It's the first impression a coach gets before they ever see you in person. If it's poorly made, too long, starts with a flashy intro instead of your best play, or makes it hard to find you on the field — you just lost your shot.

Here's how to make a highlight film that coaches will actually watch.

## The Basics: Format and Length

**Length:** 3-5 minutes. Maximum. Under no circumstances should your highlight film be longer than 5 minutes.

College coaches don't have time for a 15-minute film from an athlete they've never heard of. They'll give you 30 seconds to a minute. If you hook them in that time, they'll watch the whole thing. If you don't, they're moving on.

**Platform:** Hudl is the standard. YouTube works too. Never send a file that requires downloading — coaches won't do it.

**Quality:** HD minimum. If your film looks like it was recorded on a phone from the stands, it's not going to get taken seriously. If your school doesn't have good game film, invest in someone recording your games from the press box or a high angle.

## The First 10 Seconds Are Everything

Coaches decide within the first 10 seconds whether they're going to keep watching.

**Start with:**
- A title card (3 seconds max): Name, position, jersey number, height, weight, school, grad year, GPA
- Your single best play immediately after

**Do NOT start with:**
- A 30-second animated logo intro
- A montage set to music
- "Hey coaches, my name is..."
- Your warmup or stretching
- A play where you didn't do anything special

Your best play first. Not your second best. Not your most recent. Your absolute best play is the first thing a coach sees.

## What Plays to Include

**Include:**
- 15-25 of your best plays
- A variety of situations — not all the same play type
- Plays against quality competition (coaches can tell when you're dominating a bad team)
- Plays that show different skills:
  - For a receiver: routes, catching in traffic, after-the-catch speed, blocking
  - For a lineman: pass protection, run blocking, pulling, second-level blocks
  - For a defensive player: tackles, coverage, blitz, pursuit, turnovers
  - For a QB: reads, accuracy, pocket movement, leadership, improvisation
- A few plays that show adversity — you got beat on a play but recovered, or you made a tackle after the play broke down

**Do NOT include:**
- Plays where you can't be identified on the field
- Plays where you're standing on the sideline
- Plays where the other team clearly isn't trying
- The same play repeated 5 times from different angles
- Personal foul or unsportsmanlike penalties
- Celebration clips

## Identifying Yourself on Film

This sounds obvious but it's the most common mistake: coaches can't find you.

**Solutions:**
- Use a circle or arrow to identify yourself before each play (common on Hudl)
- Keep your jersey number visible in the title card
- If you're hard to spot, use a freeze-frame at the start of each play pointing to yourself
- Consider a split screen: wide angle on one side, zoomed on you on the other

If a coach has to pause and search for you on every play, they're closing the video.

## Film Quality Tips

**Camera angle:** High angle is best — press box, top of bleachers, or a drone shot. Sideline-level film is hard for coaches to evaluate because you can't see the full play develop.

**Audio:** Turn down the music. Coaches want to hear the game — whistles, calls, contact. If you add music, keep it low. Better yet, leave the game audio.

**Editing:** Clean cuts between plays. No fancy transitions, no slow-motion unless you're showing a replay of a particularly impressive play. Keep it simple and professional.

**Color grading and filters:** Don't. This isn't Instagram. Keep it natural.

## Position-Specific Tips

### Quarterbacks
Coaches want to see decision-making, not just arm strength.
- Show pre-snap reads (if visible on film)
- Include completions of various distances and types
- Show at least one play where you improvised and made something happen
- Show your footwork and pocket presence
- Include game management plays, not just touchdowns

### Running Backs
- Show vision and patience, not just speed
- Include plays where you break tackles
- Show pass-catching ability
- Include blitz pickup/blocking
- Show burst through the hole AND open-field running

### Wide Receivers / Tight Ends
- Route running variety (in, out, deep, crossing)
- Catches in traffic — not just wide-open touchdowns
- Blocking on run plays (coaches love receivers who block)
- Contested catches
- After-the-catch ability

### Offensive Linemen
- Pass protection reps (show technique, not just the QB having time)
- Drive blocks on run plays
- Pulling and getting to the second level
- Recovery — show a play where you got beat initially but adjusted
- Finish — coaches want to see you pancake someone

### Defensive Players
- Tackles for loss and sacks (if applicable)
- Pursuit plays — chasing down a play from the backside
- Coverage skills (if LB or DB)
- Physicality at the point of attack
- Turnovers — interceptions, forced fumbles

## Common Mistakes That Kill Highlight Films

1. **Too long.** If it's over 5 minutes, you're including plays that aren't impressive enough.
2. **Weak opening.** Your first play should make a coach say "oh, okay."
3. **Can't find the player.** If you're not highlighted, coaches move on.
4. **Only shows one skill.** A running back who only shows speed tells coaches nothing about his vision, power, or receiving ability.
5. **Only blowout games.** Coaches discount highlights against clearly inferior teams.
6. **Bad music choices.** Keep it clean. A coach is watching this at work.
7. **Sending a download link.** Hudl link or YouTube link. That's it.
