You've got the highlight film. You've been hitting the weight room. You think you're ready for college football.

But do you actually know what coaches are evaluating when they watch your film, visit your practice, or see you at a camp?

Most families don't. They focus on the wrong things — star ratings, combine numbers, who else is interested. Meanwhile, coaches are looking at a completely different set of criteria.

At Brand of a Champion, we've worked directly with the recruiting process for years. Our athletes are playing at Texas A&M, UCLA, Tennessee, Florida State, Utah, and Indiana. Here's what actually gets you recruited.

## 1. Film Over Everything

Every coach we've worked with says the same thing: film is king.

Not your 40 time. Not your bench press. Not your star rating on a recruiting website. FILM.

They want to see:
- **How you play in a real game** — not a scripted drill at a camp
- **How you react to adversity** — do you quit when your team is losing?
- **Your effort on every play** — not just the highlights, every single snap
- **Your technique** — footwork, hand placement, leverage, pad level

One D1 defensive coordinator told us: "I can teach a kid technique. I can't teach him to care. Show me a kid who plays every snap like it's his last, and I'll find a spot for him."

## 2. Size and Measurables (But Not How You Think)

Yes, size matters. But it's not as simple as "be 6'3 and 230 pounds."

Coaches look at:
- **Frame** — Can your body carry more weight? A 6'2 kid at 185 with a big frame projects differently than a 6'2 kid at 185 who's maxed out.
- **Position-specific measurables** — A running back at 5'10 200 is fine. A corner at 5'10 is fine. An offensive tackle at 5'10 is a problem.
- **Growth potential** — How old are you? A junior who's still growing is more valuable than a senior who's peaked.

**What you can control:** Your body fat percentage, your strength-to-weight ratio, and your conditioning. Show up to camps looking like an athlete, not just a big body.

## 3. Speed Is Relative

Everyone talks about 40 times. Coaches care about football speed, which is different.

Football speed means:
- How fast do you play in pads?
- How quickly do you diagnose and react?
- Can you change direction without losing speed?
- Do you play fast in the fourth quarter or just the first?

A kid who runs a 4.6 forty but plays fast on film is more recruitable than a kid who runs a 4.4 but looks slow in games. Sounds counterintuitive, but coaches see it all the time.

## 4. Football IQ Separates Good From Great

The athletes who get recruited to the highest levels aren't just talented — they're smart.

Coaches look for:
- **Pre-snap reads** — Does the linebacker recognize formations? Does the quarterback read the defense?
- **Adjustments** — When something goes wrong, does the player adapt or repeat the same mistake?
- **Awareness** — Does he know the situation? Down, distance, time, score?
- **Communication** — Is he talking to teammates, directing traffic, making calls?

At Brand of a Champion, football IQ is a core part of our development program. Physical talent gets you noticed. Mental ability gets you recruited.

## 5. Character Is a Dealbreaker

This is the one most families underestimate.

Coaches will not recruit a talented kid with character issues. Period. The risk isn't worth it.

They evaluate character by:
- **Calling your high school coach** — "Is he coachable? Does he show up? Is he a good teammate?"
- **Watching your sideline behavior** — Do you celebrate with teammates or sulk when you come off the field?
- **Checking your social media** — Every major program has a compliance staff that reviews recruits' social media. One bad tweet can kill a scholarship offer.
- **Talking to your teachers and other coaches** — Character extends beyond the football field.

**Our advice:** Be the kid coaches want in their locker room, not just on their field.

## 6. Grades Are Non-Negotiable

We covered this in our recruiting guide, but it bears repeating: no grades, no eligibility, no scholarship.

The minimum for NCAA D1 is a 2.3 GPA in core courses. But here's the reality — most competitive programs want 2.8 or above. Academic admits are easier for coaches to get through admissions.

If your GPA is borderline, you're making the coach's job harder. And coaches have 30 other recruits who don't come with academic headaches.

## 7. How You Respond to Coaching

At camps, coaches aren't just timing your 40. They're teaching you something and watching how you respond.

They want to see:
- Do you listen the first time or need to be told three times?
- Do you try to implement the coaching immediately?
- Do you get frustrated or get better?
- Do you ask questions?

One of our athletes, DJ Bordeaux — now at UCLA — stood out at a camp not because he was the most talented guy there, but because every time a coach gave him a correction, he applied it on the next rep. That's what separates recruitable athletes from talented ones.

## 8. Your Support System Matters

Coaches recruit families, not just players. They want to know:
- Are the parents supportive or problematic?
- Does the family understand the process?
- Will this kid have a stable support system in college?
- Are the parents going to be calling the coach every week complaining about playing time?

Parents: your role in the recruiting process is to be supportive, organized, and let your kid compete. The worst thing you can do is be the parent that coaches warn each other about.

## 9. Production Over Potential

Here's a hard truth: college coaches at the highest level recruit production, not potential.

Potential is "he could be good if..." Production is "he IS good."

They want to see:
- Stats that show consistency, not one big game
- Improvement from sophomore to junior year
- Performance against quality competition
- Playoff and big-game production

If you dominate weak teams but disappear against good competition, coaches notice. Our Behind The Brand documentary series shows our athletes competing in some of the toughest games in Georgia — against programs like Westlake, Carrollton, Buford, and North Cobb. That's the kind of film that gets attention.

## 10. The X-Factor: Does He Love Football?

This is the intangible that separates the scholarship athletes from the rest. Coaches can tell when a kid genuinely loves the game versus when he's playing because his parents want him to.

The kid who loves football:
- Shows up early to practice
- Watches film on his own
- Trains in the offseason without being told
- Talks about the game with passion, not obligation

Coaches want players who are going to grind through the difficult transition from high school to college football. That only happens if the love is real.
